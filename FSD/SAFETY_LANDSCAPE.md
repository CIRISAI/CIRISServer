# FSD — Safety Landscape: how decentralized networks handle moderation, CSAM, and the E2EE tension

**Companion to:** `FSD/MODERATION_CHILD_SAFETY.md` (the CIRIS design + the named-moderator existence invariant + the anti-predator audit).
**Status:** Research / comparative survey + honest assessment. No production code.
**Purpose:** Ground the CIRIS moderation design in an honest, cited reading of how every comparable network actually handles moderation, CSAM/child-safety, and the encryption-vs-detection tension — so the design's claims are calibrated against the real state of the art, not against a strawman.

The single organizing insight, stated up front and applied throughout: **detection is only possible where content is readable.** Every project resolves the CSAM problem by either (a) keeping content public / server-readable (so hash-matching or review is trivial) or (b) making content unreadable via E2EE and then refusing to scan — accepting that the only detection point would be on-device-pre-encryption, which they reject as a backdoor. **No one has squared this circle.** CIRIS does not either; what it does differently is structural (see §3).

---

## 1. The CIRIS framing this is measured against

CIRIS's public position (`https://ciris.ai/safety-vs-censorship/`) is that you moderate *without* censoring by separating crowdsourced **rules** from machined **verdicts**, gated by an **operational-language** test, with **recused appeals**. Quoting it directly:

- **Rules are crowdsourced; verdicts are machined.** "People propose and vote on rules: public, dated, signed, reversible."
- **The operational-language gate.** A rule must be "checkable without judgment, or it isn't ready." The drift it bars: a rule about "uses the wrong word for therapy" stays mechanical; one that "feels disrespectful" reintroduces interpretation and is not ready.
- **Deterministic verdicts.** "Same response + same rule → same verdict, every time." Debate moves upstream (should the rule exist?) not downstream (case-by-case interpretation).
- **Reconsideration with recusal.** Appeals go to "Reconsideration by a fresh review group (the original adjudicators recused), not back to the same crowd that produced the verdict."
- **The named risk, honestly.** "The same machinery that catches real failures can become the machinery that enforces preferences." The discipline aims to make preference-drift "visible and expensive when it happens."

That gate — *mechanically checkable rules + deterministic verdicts + recused appeals* — is **how CIRIS claims to moderate without censoring.** The comparative question below is: which of the surveyed networks even *has* a rules/verdicts separation, and which permit unmoderated spaces, and where can CSAM actually be detected. The CIRIS-specific structural claim — **no unmoderated multi-party space can exist** — is the named-moderator existence invariant (`MODERATION_CHILD_SAFETY.md` §A); this doc establishes that *no surveyed network makes that claim*, and then assesses honestly where CIRIS is genuinely different vs. where it inherits the same unsolved problem.

## 4. The regulatory asymmetry (the whole game)

The legal landscape is the frame the technical choices are made inside:

- **US (§2258A):** a **reactive** duty — report apparent CSAM "as soon as reasonably possible after obtaining actual knowledge" — and subsection (f) **explicitly bars** reading it as a scanning mandate ("Nothing in this section shall be construed to require a provider to … affirmatively search, screen, or scan"). So a CIRIS operator who *gains knowledge* (e.g. via a hash-match at the share seam) **must report**; a CIRIS operator is **not** legally required to *scan private content*.
- **EU (CSAR / "chat control"):** the attempt to add a **proactive** scanning mandate (including client-side scanning of E2EE) — which, as of late 2025, the Council retreated from (mandatory detection orders removed, "encryption cannot be weakened or bypassed" added) but **left a review clause** that does not permanently foreclose it. Not final.
- **Apple (2021–2022):** built and then **abandoned** exactly the client-side-scanning mechanism CSAR contemplated, concluding it "opens the door for bulk surveillance" and creates new threat vectors. The most load-bearing precedent that client-side scanning of private content is not a free lunch.
- **OECD (2025):** even *voluntary* transparency/detection is unevenly implemented (fewer than 60% of Tech Coalition firms use classifiers for novel material; many CSEA-intensive services report nothing).

**The implication for CIRIS:** the design's choice to run hash-matching at the **share/publish seam** (where content becomes readable and the operator can *gain knowledge* → §2258A reporting duty) and to **not** mandate client-side scanning of structurally-invisible self/family content is *exactly* the posture the legal asymmetry rewards: it satisfies the reactive US duty at the point knowledge is gained, and it does not build the bulk-surveillance backdoor Apple abandoned and CSAR retreated from. This is a deliberate, defensible position — not a gap that was overlooked.

## 5. Honest assessment — where CIRIS is genuinely different, and where it is not

The maintainer asked for "you tell me," not cheerleading. Here it is, both sides.

### 5.1 Where CIRIS is genuinely, structurally different (the real edge)

1. **No unmoderated multi-party space can exist — and that is unique in this table.** Every surveyed network permits a multi-party space with no accountable moderator: an accept-everything Nostr relay, an orphaned Matrix room, an absentee-admin Mastodon instance, a Signal/Session/Briar group, the IPFS default. CIRIS's **named-moderator existence invariant** (`MODERATION_CHILD_SAFETY.md` §A) makes the unmoderated multi-party space *structurally non-existent*: existence is gated on a named, owner-bound moderator; auto-promotion-by-`moderation_track_record` prevents any transient gap; fail-secure quiesces a group that cannot name one. **Every other network in this table fails *open*; CIRIS fails *secure*.** This is the single clearest place CIRIS is not just "better tooled" but categorically different — it removes the highest-leverage predator structure (the unmoderated group) from the protocol's vocabulary.

2. **A delegable, accountable, *signed* duty — not a role and not a void.** The networks cluster into two failure shapes: a **single operator** with unaccountable discretion (relay/instance/SOGS admin — Nostr, Mastodon, Session), or **no one at all** (Signal/Briar/IPFS). CIRIS's moderation is a `delegates_to`-carried *duty* (CEG §11.10, enforced admission): every action is **delegate-signed, traceable up the chain to an owner-bound human, and instantly revocable.** "Takedown isn't a coup" is structural, not a promise. No surveyed network has this attribution+revocability+accountability triple; most have either anonymous operator power or nothing.

3. **Trust-propagation gives moderation reach without a central authority.** The duty propagates along the trust graph (self → agent → trusted parties → community), so a community is moderated by people the community actually trusts — not by a distant instance admin (Mastodon's "can't affect another server" gap) and not by no one (Briar). This is the constructive answer to the federation-fragmentation problem the others suffer (defederation cascades, relay islands).

4. **The operational-language anti-censorship gate is a genuine answer to the over-removal half of the problem — which the others largely ignore.** Most surveyed networks treat moderation as *removal power* and address censorship only by *exit* (run your own relay/instance/labeler). Bluesky's composable labelers are the closest analogue (pluralism by subscription), but labels are still each operator's discretion. CIRIS is the only one that constrains the *content* of moderation rules: **crowdsourced, public, signed, reversible rules that must pass a mechanical-checkability test, producing deterministic verdicts, with recused appeals.** That is a real structural defense against the "machinery that catches failures becoming machinery that enforces preferences" — and it directly contains the **colluding-moderator** vector (a captured moderator can't make abuse rule-compliant without a public, voted rule-change, and can't sit on their own appeal). This is CIRIS's distinctive answer to *censorship*, the half of the problem the privacy-maximalist networks (Signal/Briar) "solve" only by having no moderation at all.

### 5.2 Where CIRIS inherits the same unsolved problem as everyone (no overclaiming)

1. **The structurally-invisible self/family core is E2EE-equivalent, and hash-matching cannot reach it — exactly as on Signal, SimpleX private chats, Briar, and Matrix encrypted rooms.** `cohort_scope: self|family` content never emits `holds_bytes:*` and never federates (CEG §10.1.4); a predator's own device or a fully-colluding household is, by construction, a place the substrate cannot scan. CIRIS does **not** solve the private-content CSAM problem. **No one in this table has** — and the field's own verdict (Apple's abandonment, the EU's retreat, §2258A's no-scan clause) is that the only "solution," client-side scanning of private content, is a bulk-surveillance backdoor that creates more harm than it prevents. CIRIS deliberately does **not** build it. The honest framing: CIRIS narrows the unmoderated/undetectable surface to *exactly* the irreducible E2EE-equivalent core and no further — but that core is real, and the containment there is the same as everywhere: detection at the *share/publish* boundary, the still-observable metadata/coordination layer (CIRIS keeps relationship-existence and graph metadata visible — §10.1.4 is content-confidentiality only — which is a genuine lever the pure-E2EE messengers partly lack), and out-of-band law-enforcement / NCMEC process. That is containment, not a cure.

2. **Grooming detection is probabilistic and adversarial — unsolved network-wide.** CIRIS's `detection:*`/`ratchet:flag:*` advisory layer over the observable metadata is a real lever (and uniquely lands on an *accountable named human* rather than no one), but sophisticated actors throttle under thresholds, migrate off-fabric, and groom in invisible scope. CIRIS's edge is "there is always someone the flag lands on," not "the flag catches everyone." No network has solved this.

3. **Age assurance bottoms out in `self`-level (unfalsifiable from inside) and verified levels depend on out-of-fabric verifiers + a non-abusive guardian.** Same trust-anchor and coerced-guardian residuals everyone faces; CIRIS narrows but does not eliminate them.

4. **The detection adapter and several dimensions are not yet shipped** (concrete PDQ matcher is out-of-tree; age/rating dimensions are fabric-side-over-`scores` workarounds). CIRIS's *trait* and *delegation spine* ship (CEG §11.5 trait, §11.10 enforced scopes); the concrete CSAM matcher is the same access-gated-hash-DB problem (PhotoDNA restricted, PDQ open) every operator faces — CIRIS does not have privileged access to NCMEC/IWF feeds.

### 5.3 Bottom line

**CIRIS is uniquely suited on the *governance* axis and inherits the universal limit on the *private-content detection* axis.**

- On governance — *who is accountable, can an unmoderated space exist, and can moderation itself be weaponized* — CIRIS is genuinely, structurally different from everything in this table: the **named-moderator existence invariant** (no unmoderated multi-party space, fail-secure), the **delegable-accountable-signed-revocable duty** ("not a coup"), **trust-propagation** (reach without a central authority), and the **operational-language anti-censorship gate** (deterministic verdicts + recused appeals) together form a posture no surveyed network has. The unmoderated-group vector — the predators' highest-leverage structure — is closed at the protocol level. The colluding-moderator vector is contained by attribution + public mechanical rules + recused appeals + canonical/accord authority. These are real, defensible, and as far as this survey found, unmatched.

- On detection — *can you find CSAM in genuinely private content* — CIRIS is **exactly as limited as every E2EE/private-content system**, and it says so. It detects at the share/publish boundary (where the legal duty also attaches), keeps the metadata/coordination layer observable (a partial lever), and relies on out-of-band process for the irreducible private core. It does **not** solve what Apple, the EU, and the entire E2EE messenger field have concluded is unsolvable without a bulk-surveillance backdoor — and it deliberately declines to build that backdoor.

The defensible claim is therefore narrow and true: **CIRIS removes the unmoderated-multi-party-space and unaccountable-moderation failure modes that every comparable network leaves open, while inheriting — and honestly naming — the same private-content detection limit they all share.** It is "uniquely suited" to the part of the problem that is a *governance* problem; it is *not* a magic solution to the part that is an *encryption* problem, and the design must never be represented as one.
